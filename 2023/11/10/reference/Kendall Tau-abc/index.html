THE ESTIMATION AND COMPARISON OF STRENGTHS OF ASSOCIATION IN CONTINGENCY TABLES

THE ESTIMATION AND COMPARISON OF STRENGTHS OF ASSOCIATION IN CONTINGENCY TABLES

BY A. STUART

Division of Research Techniques, London School of Economics

1. INTRODUCTION

In testing the significance of an observed association between two characteristics in a con-
tingency table, $\chi^2$ is generally used. When, however, we wish to measure strength of associa-
tion, some text-books recommend the use of Karl Pearson’s coefficient of contingency,
a function of $\chi^2$ which may be referred to the conventional range (- 1, + 1), although it
cannot attain the limits of this range. Kendall (1948a, chapter 13) gives references to the
work on the coefficient, largely by Karl Pearson himself, and points out the difficulty of
obtaining its sampling variance on the hypothesis of independence.

Yates (1948) proposed a test based on scores for association of characteristics of the kind
considered in this paper, but its distribution, as he points out, is only obtained on the hypo-
thesis of independence, and it becomes progressively more inaccurate as the degree of
association increases. Williams (1952) has recently considered various tests based on scores.

In this paper, a coefficient is proposed which is independent of scoring systems. It is, in
fact, a suitably modified form of Kendall’s rank correlation coefficient, which depends only
on ordinal properties. It is shown how the existing theory of the coefficient may be used to
estimate the population association, to set confidence limits for it, and also to test the
difference in the coefficients calculated for two contingency tables.

Since the proposed coefficient depends on rank order, a condition for its applicability is
that the rows and columns of the contingency table fall into a natural order. This is, in fact,
genierally the case. From one point of view the proposed coefficient may therefore be
regarded as availing itself of nmore of the information supplied by the contingency table than
does the contingency coefficient, which is invariant under interchanges of rows or columns.

The confidence interval and the significance test obtained by use of the proposed coefficient
are conservative, in the sense that a lower boundl is provided for the confidence coefficient,
and an upper bound for the probability of wrongly rejecting the hypothesis tested. This is
a consequence of the fact that onlv an upper bound is known for the sampling variance of the
coefficient, and this is in general a poor upper bound, altlhough sometimes attainable.
However, a conservative test is better than no test at all, and this test should be useful in
many practical situations, especially where samnple sizes are large.

2. CONTrNGENCY TABLES AND RANKINGS

Kendall (1949) has suggested that his rank correlation coefficient t may be calculated for
a contingency table whose rows and columns are ordered by the criteria of classification.
An $r \times s$ table with a grand total of n is regarded as two rankings of n objects according to
characteristics for one of which only r separate ranks are distinguished, and for the other
only s separate ranks. Looked at in this way, the marginal totals are simply the numbers of
objects tied at each level. There is thuis a one-one correspondence between any contingency
table and a pair of rankings.

The original context of this suggestion was the estimation of tlle product-moment
correlation parameter in the case of non-normal variationl, but there is no reason why the
coefficient t should not be used in its own right as a measure of association for contingency
tables in which there is a natuiral order for rows and columns.

3. THE DENOMINATOR OF $t$

The test commonly used to test the significance of an observed value of $t$, where there are
no ties in the rankings, is described by Kendall (1948b). Like the $\chi^2$ test for a contingency
table, it is made with reference to a hypothetical population obtained by the permutation
of sample observations. In calculating the coefficient, we nmay therefore use Daniels’s (1944)
definition of t in the universe of sample permutations. If $r_i$, $r_j$ are the ranks allotted to the
$i^{th}$ and $j^{th}$ objects $(i<j)$, and $a_{ij}$, $b_{ij}$ are scores, one defined on each ranking, given by
$$
a_{ij}, b_{ij}=
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
+1 & (r_i<r_j) \
-1 & (r_i>r_j)
\end{cases}
\nonumber
\end{equation}
$$
this definition is
$$
t=\frac{\sum a_{ij}b_{ij}}{(\sum a_{ij}^2)\frac{1}{2}(\sum b_{ij}^2)\frac{1}{2}}
\tag 1
$$(1)
The summations in (1) extend over all unequal suffixes. The property of the coefficient
$$
-1 \le t \le +1 \tag 2
$$

is seen from (1) to be a consequence of the Cauchy inequality, the equalities in (2) being
attainable only when the members of each set of scores are all + 1 or all - 1. When there
are no ties, (1) is equivalent to the more normal definition
$$
t = \frac{2S}{n(n-1)}
\tag 3
$$
where S is a function of the number of inversions formed by the two rankings.
When we come to consider rankings containing ties, we must further define
$$
a_{ij}, b_{ij} = 0 \quad (r_i = r_j )
$$

and alternative forms are now possible for $t$, for although it remains true that $\sum a_{ij}b_{ij} = 2S$, the denominators of( 1) and (3) no longer agree. If we are inter ested simply in testing whether association exists, the discussion of which form of denorninator to use for t is unnecessary: the test may be carried out directly on the numerator. But if we are concerned to measure
association, it is of some importance to choose a denominator so that the limits of the conventional range (-1, + 1) are at least sometimes attainable. With this in mind, we may now examine the possibilities.

(a) If, when there are ties present, we continue to use $n(n - 1)$ as the denominator, as in (3), we obtain the coefficient denoted by Kendall (1948b) as $t_a$.

(b) If, on the other hand, we continue to use (1), the coefficient is denoted by $t_b$. The fact that some of the $a_{ij}$, $b_{ij}$ are zero implies that the denominator is decreased in the tied, as compared to the untied, case. Nevertheless, it follows from the Cauchy inequality that the equalities in (2) are not attainable by $t_b$ except in the trivial case when all the scores are zero. It further follows that since $t_a$ has the same numerator but a larger denominator than $t_b$, and $t_b$ cannot attain + 1, $t_a$ certainly cannot attain + 1. (This is analogous to the similar deficiency of the contingency coefficient.) Where the number of ties is large, the deficiency will be serious. Since we are particularly interested in contingency tables, which often have large marginal totals, we must consider whether a more appropriate form of denominator exists.

(c) Consider the maximum (positive or negative) numerator $\sum a_{ij} b_{ij}$ which can be produced by a sample of n observations arranged in a $r \times s$ table. This will be attained when all observations lie in cells in a longest diagonal of the table, and the frequencies in these cells are equal, or as nearly equal as possible. Any move away from this position will decrease the total score $\sum a_{ij} b_{ij}$. A longest diagonal contains $m$ cells, where $m$ is the lesser of $r$ and $s$

If $n$ is a multiple of $m$, the maximum sample score is
$$
\sum a_{ij}b_{ij}=2(\frac{n}{m})^2{1+2+\cdots+(m-1)}=n^2(\frac{m-1}{m}).\tag 4
$$
(If $n = m$, we are back to the untied case, and (4) reduces to ￥n(n - 1)￥, as it should.) Thus
(4) is an attainable upper bound for $\sum a_{ij}b_{ij}$.

When $n$ is not a multiple of $m$, (4) renmains an upper bound, but cannot be attained. This
is a quite unimportant deficiency in practice, when $n$ is often large and m very small, and the
residue of n to modulus m necessarily small.
We therefore define
$$
t_c=\frac{2\sum a_{ij}b_{ij}}{n^2\frac{(m-1)}{m}}=\frac{2S}{n^2\frac{(m-1)}{m}} \tag 5
$$

whence
$$
t_c=\frac{(n-1)}{n}\frac{m}{(m-1)}t_a \tag 6
$$
It follows that $t_c$ can sometimes attain, and for large n can generally almost attain, $\pm$ 1.

4. THE SAMPLING PROPERTIES OF $t_c$

Remembering the correspondence between a pair of rankings and a contingency table, let
us consider the cell frequencies of an $r \times s$ table,
$$
f_{ik} \quad (i=1,\cdots,r;k=1,\cdots,8),
$$
as having been sampled from a population $r \times s$ table with frequencies $F_{ik}. Then
$$
t_a=\frac{1}{n(n-1)}\sum a_{ij}b_{kl}f_{ik}f_{il}
$$
and
$$
E(t_a) = \frac{1}{n(n-1)}\sum a_{ij}b_{kl}E(f_{ik}f_{jl}),
$$
which, by the ordinary formula for sampling from a finite multinomial population, becomes
$$
= \frac{1}{n(n-1)} \sum a_{ij}b_{kl} \frac{n(n-1)}{N(N-1)}F_{ik}F_{jl} \
=\frac{1}{N(N-1)}\sum a_{ij}b_{kl} F_{ik}F_{jl}=\tau_a
$$

where $tau_a$ is defined for the population exactly as is $t_a$ for the sample. Thus $t_a$ is unbiased
for $\tau_a$.
